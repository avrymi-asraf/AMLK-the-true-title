❯ explain me this: "I went looking for a hyperparameter problem and found a data problem. completion_only_loss=True maximizes log P(reference | article) — and gradient descent has no idea whether the reference is actually derivable from the article. Measured on your test set with generous Hebrew stemming:"

Sure. There are three separate claims packed in there. Let me unpack each.

1. What completion_only_loss=True actually does

Your training examples are (prompt, completion) pairs — the prompt is the instruction plus the article, the completion is the reference summary. The model is a next-token predictor. Training shows it the whole sequence and asks: at each position, what's the probability you assign to the token that actually comes next?

completion_only_loss=True says: only count the loss on the completion tokens. Don't penalize the model for being bad at predicting the article's own tokens — it's not being asked to write the article, it's being asked to write the summary. So the loss is computed only over the reference summary's tokens, conditioned on everything before them (the instruction and the article).

That's a good setting. It's the right thing to do. Written out, the objective is:

▎ maximize, over every training example, the probability the model assigns to the reference summary's tokens given the article that precedes them.

That's what "maximizes log P(reference | article)" means. P(reference | article) is the probability the model puts on producing that exact summary after reading that article. log is there because you sum log-probabilities instead of multiplying probabilities (numerically saner, same argmax).
